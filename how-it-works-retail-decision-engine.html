The Technology

How each AI model works

Each model is chosen for a specific reason — not just because it's popular, but because it's the right tool for the problem.

📈

Module 1

Holt-Winters Exponential Smoothing — Demand Forecasting

Holt-Winters is a classical time series model that decomposes sales history into three components: level (the baseline), trend (is demand growing or shrinking?), and seasonality (how much does demand fluctuate by week or month?). Unlike black-box deep learning models, every component is interpretable — you can see exactly why the model predicts higher demand in December vs. July. It's trained on 104 weeks of historical sales data per product and outputs a point forecast plus a 95% confidence interval for each future week. The confidence interval is critical for inventory planning: ordering to the upper bound protects against stockouts; ordering to the lower bound minimizes holding costs.

statsmodels · Additive seasonality · 52-week seasonal period
💰

Module 2

Price Elasticity + SciPy Optimization — Dynamic Pricing

Price elasticity measures how sensitive customers are to price changes. A product with elasticity of −1.8 means: raise the price 10% and demand drops 18%. The elasticity is estimated from historical price and sales data. Once known, the profit function — (price − cost) × demand(price) — becomes a continuous curve with a single peak. SciPy's bounded scalar minimizer finds this peak mathematically in milliseconds. The key insight: this is not a lookup table or a heuristic — it's a mathematical proof that no other price generates more weekly profit given the estimated elasticity. The live price slider lets users see in real time how far their current price is from the optimum.

scipy.optimize · Price elasticity model · Real-time profit curve
📦

Module 3

OR-Tools CP-SAT Solver — Inventory Optimization

The inventory problem is fundamentally different from pricing — it's a discrete, multi-period decision with hard constraints. For each of the next 8 weeks, the solver must decide: order or not order, and if so, how many units? The constraints include: stock must never fall below the minimum safety level, stock cannot exceed warehouse capacity, and there is a lead time between ordering and receiving. CP-SAT models this as a mixed-integer optimization problem — 630 binary and integer variables — and finds the combination that minimizes total cost (order fixed cost + per-unit cost + holding cost + shortage penalty). It also calculates the EOQ (Economic Order Quantity) as a reference benchmark from classical inventory theory.

OR-Tools CP-SAT · Multi-period planning · EOQ benchmark

How They Connect

From data to decision in 4 steps

The three models are not independent — they feed into each other to produce a single integrated recommendation.

1

Historical sales → Holt-Winters fits seasonal pattern

104 weeks of weekly sales data is decomposed into trend + seasonal components. The model is fit separately for each product because each has a different seasonal pattern — milk peaks in winter, orange juice peaks in summer.

model = ExponentialSmoothing(
  weekly_sales, trend="add", seasonal="add",
  seasonal_periods=52
)
forecast = model.fit().forecast(8)  # next 8 weeks
2

Price elasticity → SciPy finds the profit-maximizing price

The demand function D(price) = base_demand × (price/base_price)^elasticity is combined with the profit formula to create a continuous profit curve. SciPy searches this curve for its peak — the optimal price — in under 1 millisecond.

# Profit = (price - cost) × demand(price)
def profit(p):
  d = base_demand × (p/base_price) ** elasticity
  return -(p - unit_cost) × d

result = minimize_scalar(profit, bounds=(min_p, max_p))
3

Forecast demand → OR-Tools plans 8-week order schedule

The 8-week demand forecast from Module 1 is passed directly into the inventory optimizer as the demand input. The solver then determines the cheapest combination of order quantities and timing that keeps stock within safe levels throughout the planning horizon.

# Minimize: order costs + holding costs
model.Minimize(
  Σ(order_flag[t] × order_cost)
  + Σ(order_qty[t] × unit_cost)
  + Σ(stock[t] × holding_cost)
)
4

Summary Dashboard integrates all 3 into one action

The Summary Dashboard combines outputs from all three modules for every product and presents them side-by-side: this week's demand forecast vs. last week, the recommended price adjustment and its profit impact, and whether to place an order this week and how many units. One page, three products, complete picture.

Business Applications

How retailers can use this today

Every decision this system makes replaces a manual process that currently takes hours — or doesn't happen at all. Here are six concrete applications.

🥛
Grocery / Perishables

Perishable Waste Reduction

Perishable products like milk, bread, and fresh produce have a shelf life of days. Overordering leads to waste and markdowns; underordering leads to empty shelves and lost sales. The demand forecast tells a store manager exactly how much to order each week, accounting for seasonal patterns (milk sells more in winter, juice in summer) and day-of-week peaks (Saturday sells 40% more than Tuesday). The inventory optimizer then calculates the precise order quantity that minimizes waste while avoiding stockouts — a calculation that would take a manager hours to do manually for every SKU.

Expected: 15–25% reduction in perishable waste
🏷️
Grocery / Pricing

Weekly Price Optimization

Most small and mid-size grocery chains set prices once a quarter based on competitor surveys and gut feeling. The dynamic pricing module shows that even a $0.20 price adjustment on a high-velocity item like milk can shift weekly profit by hundreds of dollars. By calculating the optimal price for each product each week — factoring in cost, demand sensitivity, and competitive positioning — the engine enables data-driven weekly price reviews that take minutes instead of days.

Expected: 8–15% improvement in gross margin per SKU
📅
Grocery / Operations

Seasonal Inventory Planning

Orange juice demand spikes in December and January; bread demand rises in November. Without forecasting, buyers either overstock at peak (wasting capital on inventory) or understock (missing the seasonal opportunity). The 8-week planning horizon in the inventory optimizer gives buyers enough lead time to build up stock before the seasonal peak and run it down cleanly afterward — matching inventory investment precisely to expected demand rather than to last year's intuition.

Expected: 20–30% reduction in peak-season overstock
🏪
Convenience Stores

Multi-SKU Category Management

A convenience store manager typically oversees hundreds of SKUs with limited time for analysis. The product-by-product tab structure of the Retail Decision Engine mirrors how a category manager thinks — one product at a time, with all the relevant data in one view. Scaling this system to cover 50 or 100 products would give a category manager a complete weekly decision brief: which prices to adjust, which products to order, and which categories are trending up or down — all without opening a spreadsheet.

Expected: 3–4 hours saved per buyer per week
🚚
Wholesale / Distribution

Supplier Order Consolidation

Wholesalers and distributors place orders from multiple suppliers with different lead times and minimum order quantities. The inventory optimizer's 8-week planning horizon makes it possible to consolidate orders across products supplied by the same vendor — reducing the number of individual purchase orders, qualifying for volume discounts, and smoothing delivery schedules. The EOQ benchmark provides a principled starting point for negotiating minimum order quantities with suppliers.

Expected: 10–20% reduction in order processing costs
📊
Retail Analytics / Consulting

Data-Driven Buyer Training

The real-time price slider and live profit curve make the relationship between price, demand, and profit viscerally clear — even to non-technical buyers. Many retail organizations struggle to get buyers to adopt data-driven pricing because the models feel like black boxes. The Retail Decision Engine's interactive visualizations make the logic transparent: move the price slider and watch the profit curve update in real time. This transparency builds trust in the model and accelerates adoption across the buying team.

Expected: Faster adoption of analytical decision-making across teams

Why It Matters

What this demonstrates

🔗

Systems Thinking

Connecting three different AI models — time series, continuous optimization, and combinatorial optimization — into a single coherent product requires understanding how each model's output becomes another model's input. This end-to-end design thinking is what separates a data scientist from a data product builder.

🧰

Right Tool for Each Problem

Holt-Winters for time series (interpretable, fast, proven), SciPy for continuous optimization (mathematically exact), OR-Tools for discrete scheduling (provably optimal). Using the right tool for each problem — rather than forcing one tool to do everything — is a hallmark of experienced engineering.

🎨

UX for Non-Technical Users

The live price slider, color-coded charts, and plain-language Summary Dashboard are designed for a store manager, not a data scientist. Making complex AI outputs immediately actionable for non-technical decision-makers is a product management skill as much as an engineering one.

🌺

Directly Applicable to Hawaii Retail

Hawaii's grocery and convenience retail market operates with thin margins, high transportation costs, and unique seasonal patterns (tourist peaks, local festivals). The combination of demand forecasting, optimal pricing, and inventory minimization addresses exactly the pressures that Hawaii retailers face — making this not just a portfolio demo but a deployable tool.
