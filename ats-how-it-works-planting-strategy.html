Engine 01 — Technical Deep Dive

How the Planting Strategy Optimizer works

A plain-English explanation of how CP-SAT constraint programming solves the 24-month crop rotation problem for a Hawaii specialty farm.

Instead of guessing which crops to plant where and when, this engine mathematically proves the most profitable 24-month schedule — respecting parcel availability, labor capacity limits, and seasonal price swings simultaneously.

Overview

What it optimizes

6 rotational parcels. 5 crop types. 36-month planning horizon. One mathematically proven schedule.

SettingDetail

The Technology

Combinatorial optimization, not trial and error

Certifiably optimal — not a heuristic

OR-Tools CP-SAT — Constraint Programming

Google's CP-SAT solver searches the entire feasible space using constraint propagation, conflict-driven clause learning, and branch-and-bound. With a 10-second time limit, it returns the best solution found — typically OPTIMAL for this problem size. The same solver is used in Google's supply chain and logistics systems.

Seasonal price × yield × labor — all in one objective

Revenue-Maximizing Objective Function

The objective maximizes total expected revenue across the planning horizon. Each planted batch contributes: base_price × seasonal_multiplier × yield_kg. Winter months (Dec–Feb) carry +40% price premium. Summer (Jul–Aug) +20%. Off-season (Sep–Nov) −20%. The solver finds the planting schedule that captures the most high-premium harvest windows without overloading labor.

Constraint Design

Hard rules that shape the solution space

Every constraint encodes a real operational reality — not a textbook example.

HARD

Parcel exclusivity

For each parcel p and month m, at most one cyclical crop can be occupying the parcel at that time. "Occupying" means: planted at start_m where start_m ≤ m < start_m + growth + harvest_duration. AddAtMostOne over all overlapping X variables.

HARD

Monthly labor capacity

The total harvest kg in any month cannot exceed the available labor capacity. Peak seasons (Jan, Jul, Aug, Dec) allow 25,000 kg/month; off-peak allows 15,000 kg/month. This models the farm's ability to hire seasonal workers during busy periods.

HARD

Minimum annual plantings

Each cyclical crop must be planted at least once per planning year (configurable). Ensures revenue diversity and prevents the solver from concentrating entirely on one high-price crop that happens to peak in a particular season.

HARD

Max simultaneous plantings

No more than max_simul (default 3) of the same crop type can be planted in the same month across all parcels. Prevents over-concentration risk — if one crop is hit by pests or weather, the whole farm isn't affected at once.

OBJECTIVE

Perennial orchard fixed calendar

Coffee (P07–P09) and Macadamia (P10) trees are treated as fixed infrastructure. Their harvest kg and timing are read from the orchard calendar and added directly to the labor constraint — they compete for the same monthly labor capacity as rotational crops.

OBJECTIVE

Seasonal price maximization

Revenue for each harvest event = yield_kg × base_price × seasonal_multiplier(harvest_month). The solver naturally times plantings so harvests land in premium-price months. Papaya ($2.50/kg base × 1.4× = $3.50/kg in winter) is a strong winter revenue driver.

The Math

Decision variables & objective

# Decision variable X[p][c][m] ∈ {0, 1} = 1 if crop c is planted in parcel p starting at month m # Objective (maximize) Σ_{p,c,m} X[p][c][m] × yield_kg[c] × price(c, harvest_month(c, m)) # Constraint A — Parcel exclusivity ∀ parcel p, month t: Σ_{c,m: m≤t<m+duration[c]} X[p][c][m] ≤ 1 # Constraint B — Labor capacity ∀ month t: Σ_{p,c,m: harvest overlaps t} X[p][c][m] × harvest_kg[c] + orchard_fixed_harvest[t] ≤ capacity[t] # Price multiplier price_mult(month) = 1.4 (Dec–Feb) | 1.2 (Jul–Aug) | 0.8 (Sep–Nov) | 1.0 (other)

Data Pipeline

Results flow into Lifecycle Manager

The optimizer's output is not just a report — it seeds the next engine in the pipeline.

  1. Optimal schedule written to session_state

    planting_result containing schedule[], status ("OPTIMAL"/"FEASIBLE"), and total expected revenue is stored in Streamlit session_state after each run.

  2. New parcels synced to Lifecycle Manager

    For each parcel in the schedule that doesn't already have an active crop batch, a new lifecycle batch is created: batch_id, parcel_id, crop, planted_date = 2026-01-01, initial GDD. Existing mature crops are not overwritten.

  3. Perennial parcels excluded from sync

    Parcel IDs starting with "P00" (virtual planning parcels) and the fixed orchard parcels P07–P10 are excluded from the sync. Only new rotational planting decisions are passed forward.

  4. Portal dashboard reads revenue forecast

    The Farm Owner (CEO) portal reads planting_result.total_revenue as the 12-month revenue baseline, comparing it with the Yield Simulator's Monte Carlo P50 forecast.

Why It Matters

What this demonstrates

⚙️
Production-Grade Solver

OR-Tools CP-SAT returns a mathematically certified solution — not a greedy heuristic. The same approach scales to hundreds of parcels and multi-year horizons without algorithm changes.

🏝️
Hawaii Domain Encoding

Seasonal tourist price premiums, perennial orchard calendars, and crop-specific growth periods are first-class model parameters — not hardcoded assumptions. Swapping to a different farm is a data change, not a code change.

🔗
Pipeline Integration

Planting decisions don't stay on this page — they flow into the Lifecycle Manager as initial batch state, seeding the GDD tracking that drives downstream alerts and harvest scheduling.

📊
Decision Under Uncertainty

The optimizer handles deterministic planning; the Yield Simulator provides the probabilistic overlay. Together they give owners both the optimal plan and the confidence interval around it.
