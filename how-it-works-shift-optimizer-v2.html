The Technology

2 layers working together

Provably optimal — not trial and error

Constraint Programming — OR-Tools CP-SAT

Google's CP-SAT solver assigns 651 binary variables simultaneously. It doesn't try schedules one by one — it reasons over all possibilities at once using constraint propagation and branch-and-bound search. The result is a mathematically proven optimal schedule. The same solver powers Google's workforce scheduling and logistics routing.

Hard rules guaranteed + soft preferences maximized

2-Stage Objective — Penalty + Wage

The objective function has two layers. First, constraint violations (preferences, fairness, safety) are minimized with a weight of ×10,000. Second, labor cost is minimized. This guarantees that a single constraint violation will never be traded away for any amount of wage savings — compliance always comes first.

Constraint Design

Hard rules vs. soft preferences

The key engineering decision: which rules must never be broken, and which are best-effort goals?

Phase 1 — Core Constraints

HARD — Always enforced

Must-off days

Absolute unavailability. The solver will never assign a shift on these days.

HARD — Always enforced

Demand staffing minimums (day-type aware)

Each shift must meet minimum headcount — with different targets for Normal / Sale Day / Holiday. If unsatisfiable, the solver reports "no solution" rather than producing an invalid schedule.

HARD — Always enforced

Leader per shift (Manager or Section Lead)

Every single shift must have at least 1 leader (is_leader=True). Additionally, at least 1 manager-level employee must appear somewhere in the day.

HARD — Always enforced

Skill coverage — Cashier / Food Advanced / Deli Advanced

Each shift must have: Cashier Basic × N (configurable, default 2), Food Advanced × 1, Deli Advanced × 1, and Food Basic × 1. Guarantees a minimum level of skill in every shift.

HARD — Always enforced

Max consecutive working days

No employee works more than N consecutive days (configurable, default 5). Enforced via a sliding window constraint.

SOFT — Penalized if violated

Prefer-off days

Penalty: 100 × role weight. Part-timers carry weight 2.0 (penalty 200), Managers carry 0.3 (penalty 30). The solver naturally prioritizes honoring PT preferences over manager preferences.

SOFT — Penalized if violated

Shift time preference

Penalty: 50 × role weight per mismatch. Each shift assigned to an employee that doesn't match their preferred time (Morning / Midday / Evening) adds to the penalty total.

SOFT — Penalized if violated

Senior coverage

Penalty: 150 per shift. At least 1 senior employee per shift is preferred. Violations are penalized but not forbidden.

Phase 2 — Fairness & Compliance

HARD — Always enforced

Evening → Morning interval

An employee who works the Evening shift (14–22) cannot be assigned the Morning shift (6–14) the following day. Enforced as: x[e, d, evening] + x[e, d+1, morning] ≤ 1.

HARD — Always enforced

No consecutive evening shifts

No employee can work 2+ consecutive evening shifts. Prevents accumulation of late-night fatigue.

HARD — Always enforced

Weekly hours cap

Full-time staff (Manager / SL / Staff): max 40h/week = max 5 shifts. Part-time: max 20h/week = max 2–4 shifts (per employee setting). This is derived from the role and applied as a hard upper bound.

SOFT — Penalized if violated

Evening shift count equalization

Penalty: 80 × (max evening count − min evening count) across all employees. The solver minimizes the gap, preventing evening shifts from concentrating on the same people every week.

SOFT — Penalized if violated

Weekend shift equalization

Penalty: 80 × (max weekend count − min weekend count). Saturday and Sunday shifts are distributed as evenly as possible across the entire team.

SOFT — Penalized if violated

Safety score threshold

Penalty: 800 per skill per shift. If a shift's safety score (skill quality relative to target headcount) falls below the day-type threshold, a high penalty is applied. Threshold defaults: Normal ≥ 40 / Holiday ≥ 50 / Sale ≥ 55.

Shift Safety Score

Measuring skill quality per shift

A single number that answers: "Is this shift adequately staffed with skilled workers relative to how busy it needs to be?"

Safety = (Advanced × 2 + Basic × 1) ÷ (Target headcount × 2) × 100

Using target headcount (not actual headcount) as the denominator is intentional. A shift with only 2 people even if both are Advanced will score lower than a fully staffed shift — because 2 people cannot safely cover a shift that requires 6. Understaffing risk is built into the score.

🟢 ≥ threshold — Safe 🟡 ≥ threshold−15 — Caution 🔴 Below — Action required
Day Type 🟢 Safe 🟡 Caution Reason

Sale Day≥ 55≥ 40Peak demand — maximum skill depth required

Evening shifts will naturally score lower due to fewer target staff — this correctly reflects the higher risk of understaffing in the evening, not a flaw in scheduling.

Substitute Suggestion

Instant replacement when someone calls out

Select the absent day, shift, and employee. The app scans all 31 staff and returns ranked substitutes in real time — no re-optimization needed.

  1. Hard constraint filtering

    Candidates are automatically excluded if they: already have a shift that day, have a must-off day, would exceed their weekly hours cap, or would violate the evening→morning interval or consecutive evening rule.

  2. Skill match scoring

    Each candidate is scored based on how well they cover the absent employee's skill levels. Advanced matches score +2, Basic matches score +1. Skill shortfalls apply a −2 penalty per missing level.

  3. Ranked by: skill → hours slack → wage

    Candidates are sorted by (1) skill match score, (2) remaining schedule flexibility (hours below their weekly cap), then (3) hourly wage — cheaper when skill match is equal. Top 10 are shown.

  4. Soft-NG flagged, not excluded

    Candidates who prefer the day off are shown with a ⚠️ flag — they are technically available but may be reluctant. The manager makes the final call.

The Process

From preferences to optimal schedule

What happens when you click "Optimize Shifts."

  1. 651 binary variables are created

    One variable per (employee × day × shift). Value 1 = assigned, 0 = not assigned. The solver must determine all 651 simultaneously.

  2. Hard constraints are added to the model

    Each business rule becomes a mathematical inequality. Must-off days set variables to 0. Skill coverage requirements set lower bounds on sums of qualified employees per shift. Interval constraints link adjacent-day variables.

  3. Soft constraints become weighted penalty terms

    Preference violations, fairness gaps, and safety shortfalls are converted into integer penalty terms. Role weights (0.3 for Manager → 2.0 for Part-time) are applied so PT preferences naturally receive stronger protection.

  4. 2-stage objective function is minimized

    Penalty × 10,000 + wage cost. This single expression captures both compliance (dominant) and cost efficiency (secondary). The solver never trades a constraint violation for wage savings.

  5. CP-SAT solves and returns results

    Constraint propagation prunes impossible assignments early. Branch-and-bound search explores the remaining solution space. The optimal (or best feasible) schedule is returned with: satisfaction scores, evening/weekend fairness gaps, weekly hours, and total wage cost.

Results Explained

What the dashboard shows

① Overall Metrics

Total staff-shifts, Penalty Score (soft constraint violations only — 0 is ideal), Total Hours, Weekly Wage, Evening gap (max−min evening shifts across all employees), Weekend gap.

② Employee Satisfaction

Score 0–100 using the same formula for everyone: 100 − (penalty / 450 × 100). All employees are evaluated equally — role weights only affect how the optimizer allocates violations, not how satisfaction is scored.

③ Position Tabs — Bar chart + Shift table

Management / Section Lead / Staff / Part-time shown separately. Each tab shows: satisfaction bar (+ days worked line), then the full weekly schedule with evening count, weekend count, hours, and wage per person.

④ Headcount Summary

Staff count per shift per day. Cells below the demand target are flagged ⚠️. Gives a quick daily coverage overview.

⑤ Safety Score Heatmap + Table

Heatmap: skill × (day × shift) colored by safety score. Table: detailed breakdown showing Advanced × n, Basic × n per skill per shift, with 🟢🟡🔴 relative to the day-type threshold. Evening shifts will structurally score lower — this is expected and meaningful.

⑥ Substitute Suggestion

Select any shift and absent employee to get an instant ranked list of eligible replacements — filtered by hard constraints, ranked by skill match, hours slack, and wage.
