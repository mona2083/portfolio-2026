The Technology

3 AI layers working together

Each layer handles a different part of the problem. Together they form a system no single technology could achieve alone.

🧮

Layer 1

Mathematical Optimization — OR-Tools

Google's OR-Tools solves a "knapsack problem" — given a budget and dozens of options, it finds the exact combination with the highest total value. Not trial and error: a mathematical proof. The answer is the best possible answer, guaranteed. The same technology powers airline scheduling, supply chain logistics, and Google Maps routing.

Provably optimal — not just "pretty good"
⚖️

Layer 2

Utility Theory — Economics-based scoring

Utility Theory — used in behavioral finance — lets us assign a numerical "life value" to any spending. Each item scores across 4 axes: Health, Relationships, Freedom, and Growth. Your sliders set how much each axis matters. The optimizer maximizes your total utility, not just your bank balance.

Rooted in Nobel Prize-winning economic theory
🤖

Layer 3

LLM Integration — Gemini API

Google's Gemini fills in realistic cost and score estimates when you add a new item. Type "electric scooter" and it instantly returns initial cost, monthly cost, and scores for all 4 axes. It also generates a plain-language summary of your result in the tone of a lifestyle advisor.

LLM as a data assistant, not just a chatbot

The Process

From your input to optimal answer

Here's exactly what happens when you click "Optimize."

1

Your values become numbers

Your slider settings become weights in a mathematical formula. Higher weight = that axis counts more when scoring each item.

# Your priority sliders
weights = {
  "health":     9,  # you care deeply about health
  "connections": 6,
  "freedom":   4,
  "growth":    7,
  "savings":   5,
}
2

Each item gets a utility score

For every candidate item the app calculates a "utility." Higher priority items get a bonus multiplier (up to 2×) so the things you want most are strongly preferred.

# Utility = weighted sum × priority bonus
utility = (
  w["health"] × (health + 10) × 100
  + w["connections"] × connections × 100
  + w["freedom"] × freedom × 100
  + w["growth"] × growth × 100
) × priority_bonus  # 1.0× – 2.0×
3

Savings competes as a real option

Unlike a typical budgeting app that treats savings as "leftover money," this optimizer treats saving as a choice that competes directly with spending. If savings=9, the optimizer actively sacrifices items to protect your savings goal.

# Savings utility scales with total item value
savings_coef = (
  total_utility × w["savings"]
) ÷ (10 × monthly_budget)

savings_value = actual_savings × savings_coef
4

OR-Tools finds the provably best answer

The CP-SAT solver considers all possible combinations simultaneously and returns the selection that maximizes total utility + savings, subject to your budget and rules (e.g. "exactly one transport package," "pet insurance only if pet is selected").

# Maximize: item utility + savings value
model.Maximize(
  Σ(x[i] × utility[i]) + savings_value
)
# Subject to constraints
model.Add(Σ monthly_costs ≤ budget)
model.Add(Σ transport_items = 1)
