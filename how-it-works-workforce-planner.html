How the Planner works

A plain-English explanation of the Monte Carlo workforce simulation — written for hiring managers, not just engineers.

Instead of guessing whether your team can cover every shift, this app simulates hundreds of weeks of random absences — and tells you exactly where the gaps are, who to train, and whether hiring or upskilling is cheaper.

The Technology

2 engines working together

One engine generates realistic schedules. The other measures how stable they are under uncertainty.

Probabilistic — not a single worst case

Monte Carlo Simulation

Runs 500 independent schedules, each with a different random set of absent staff. The fraction of runs where every shift requirement was met — headcount, skill coverage, leader presence — becomes the coverage rate. This is far more informative than a single best-case or worst-case scenario.

Rule-based — fast and transparent

Greedy Scheduler (simple_schedule)

Fills each shift using a priority queue: leader first → Food Advanced → Deli Advanced → Cashier Intermediate × N → fill to target by shift preference. Day and shift order are randomized each simulation run, so mornings, evenings, weekdays, and sale days are all treated equally across the full distribution.

Simulation Design

How each simulation run works

Every run is independent, with different random absences and weekly preference variations.

  1. Weekly shift preferences are pre-generated

    Click "Generate Weekly Preferences" to create a realistic multi-week pattern. Managers stay fixed. Full-time staff have a 20% chance of 1-day variation per week. Part-time staff are fully randomized each week — reflecting how their availability actually changes.

  2. Random absences are drawn

    Each simulation samples absent employees based on the absence rate (default 15% — roughly 1–2 people out of 10 unexpectedly unavailable per shift). The same absence draw applies to all weeks in that run, reflecting realistic "bad week" and "good week" variance.

  3. Greedy scheduling runs for each week independently

    For each of the n weeks, simple_schedule fills shifts using the that week's preferences and the absent set. Day and shift processing order are randomly shuffled every time — preventing any single shift type from being systematically favored.

  4. Coverage is checked against all requirements simultaneously

    A shift is counted as "covered" only if ALL of these are satisfied: headcount ≥ target, ≥ 1 leader, ≥ 1 Food Advanced, ≥ 1 Deli Advanced, ≥ N Cashier Intermediate. Partial credit is not given — reflecting real operational risk.

  5. Results are aggregated across all runs and weeks

    Coverage rate = hits / total slots. Skill hit rates are tracked independently per (day type × shift × skill). This produces the heatmaps, gap tables, and training ROI scores in the dashboard.

Weekly Variation Model

Shift preferences change week to week

Real staff don't submit identical availability every week. The simulation models this realistically by role.

Fixed — All Weeks

Store Manager / Asst. Manager

Shift preferences and days-off stay identical across all weeks. Management schedules are typically set well in advance and rarely change on short notice.

Slight Variation — 20% Chance

Section Lead / Full-time Staff

Base preferences stay fixed, but each week there is a 20% chance that one day-off changes (added or removed, max 2 days total). Reflects occasional schedule adjustments for appointments or personal plans.

Fully Random — Every Week

Part-time Staff

Shift preference (morning / midday / evening / any) and days-off (0–3 days) are re-randomized from scratch each week. Part-time availability is inherently unpredictable — this is the most realistic model for how PT staff actually submit their requests.

Statistical Reliability

Why 4 weeks × 500 simulations?

Coverage rates are modeled as binomial proportions. The more samples per day type, the tighter the confidence interval.

Margin of error = ±1.96 × √(p(1−p) / n) n (samples) = simulations × days of that type × planning weeks Example — Sale Day, 4 weeks × 500 sims: n = 500 × 1 sale day/week × 4 weeks = 2,000 samples → ±2.2% precision
Planning Period Sale Day Samples Precision Verdict
1 week × 500 sims500±4.4%⚠️ Borderline
2 weeks × 500 sims1,000±3.1%✅ Acceptable
4 weeks × 500 sims2,000±2.2%✅ Recommended
4 weeks × 1,000 sims4,000±1.6%✅ High confidence

Normal and Holiday day samples are also multiplied by the planning period — 4 weeks gives 4× the statistical power of a 1-week run for every day type simultaneously.

Skill Coverage Analysis

Three coverage metrics per skill × shift

The heatmap tracks three independent rates for each (day type × shift × skill) combination.

🟢

Advanced Hit Rate

% of simulation runs where ≥ 1 Advanced holder was assigned to this shift. The primary quality signal — Advanced staff can lead and train others.

🟡

Intermediate Hit Rate

% of runs where ≥ 1 Intermediate holder was present (counted independently of Advanced). Measures backup depth — how often at least a mid-level person covers when Advanced is absent.

🔴

No-Skill Rate

% of runs where neither Advanced nor Intermediate was present. High no-skill rate = operational risk of running a shift with no qualified staff for that skill. Shown inverted (lower is better).

Skill Coverage Score (for gap table): Score = (Advanced × 2 + Intermediate × 1) ÷ (Target headcount × 2) × 100 Using target headcount as denominator is intentional: A shift with 2 Advanced staff still scores low if 6 are needed. Understaffing risk is embedded in the score.
🟢 ≥ 95% — Stable, reliably covered 🟡 80–95% — Caution, occasional gaps 🔴 < 80% — At risk, frequent shortfalls

Key Person Risk

What if this person leaves?

Re-runs the full MC simulation with each manager or section lead excluded, one at a time. Measures how much coverage drops without them.

Dependency Score = max over all shifts of: Baseline coverage − Coverage without this person Example: Baseline Morning Sale Day coverage: 78% Without MGR-01: 44% → Dependency Score: 34pt (🔴 High)
ScoreRatingAction
≥ 20pt🔴 High DependencySuccession planning or structural reinforcement needed

Only managers and section leads are analyzed — the roles where absence structurally impacts shift leadership requirements. The calculation uses half the normal simulation count to keep runtime manageable.

Training Priority

Who to train first, and in what skill

Ranks every possible training investment by cost efficiency — how much improvement per dollar spent.

Cost Efficiency Score = Shortage Severity × Deployability ÷ Training Cost × 1,000 Where: Shortage Severity = 100 − Advanced Hit Rate (how often skill was missing) Deployability = (available days/week ÷ 7) × shift preference match match: ×2.0 | any: ×1.0 | other shift: ×0.2 Training Cost = hourly wage × training hours none→Intermediate: 20h | Intermediate→Advanced: 40h Note: Score is a relative ranking. Absolute values have no independent meaning.
High Priority Signal

High Severity + High Deployability

The skill is frequently missing in that shift AND the candidate can actually cover it after training. This combination produces the highest ROI scores and should be actioned first.

Low Priority Signal

High Severity + Low Deployability

The skill is missing, but the candidate prefers a different shift or has limited availability. Training them won't fill the gap. The model correctly deprioritizes these — consider hiring instead.

If MC has not been run yet, the score falls back to a simpler safety improvement calculation using a single greedy schedule run. Run the simulation first for accurate results.

Hire vs. Train Cost

A data-driven make-or-buy decision

Compares the true total cost of two strategies for filling a skill gap.

Hiring Cost Components

New Hire Total

Job posting & advertising fee + new hire training cost + productivity loss during ramp-up period (configurable weeks × (1 − ramp rate) × daily wage). All parameters are adjustable in the sidebar.

Training Cost Components

Upskill Total

Trainee time cost (hourly wage × training hours) + trainer time cost (same rate × 20% overhead). If fewer existing candidates exist than needed, the gap is filled with additional hires and that cost is added.

Hiring cost = job_posting + new_hire_training + (wage × hours/day × ramp_weeks × (1 − ramp_rate)) Training cost = trainee_hourly × hours + trainer_hourly × hours × 0.2 Training hours: none → Intermediate: 20h | Intermediate → Advanced: 40h

Recommendation logic

If total training cost (including any extra hires for uncovered gaps) is less than total hiring cost → recommend Train. Otherwise → recommend Hire. The comparison updates live as you adjust the cost parameters in the sidebar.

Why It Matters

What this project demonstrates

🎲

Stochastic Modeling

Workforce planning is inherently probabilistic. Applying Monte Carlo simulation — rather than a single greedy schedule — reflects how real operations actually work: with variance, absences, and weekly unpredictability.

📐

Statistical Rigor

Coverage rates use binomial proportion confidence intervals. The app surfaces sample-size tradeoffs (1 week vs. 4 weeks) rather than hiding statistical uncertainty behind a single number.

🔗

Domain Knowledge Embedded

Skill coverage rules, PT weekly variation, leader-per-shift requirements, and evening interval constraints reflect real supermarket management expertise. The model encodes operational knowledge, not just generic scheduling.

💡

Actionable, Not Just Descriptive

Every output connects to a decision: which shift to reinforce, who to train, whether to hire. The training ROI ranking and hire vs. train calculator translate simulation results directly into management action.
