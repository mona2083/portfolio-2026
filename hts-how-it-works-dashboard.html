Dashboards as
Decision Architecture

Choosing what to measure, who sees what, and in what order — these are design decisions that determine whether a dashboard changes behavior or gets ignored. This page documents the reasoning behind every choice in the HR Tech Suite Control Tower.

For Executives, HR Managers, and Ops Managers: this page explains the architecture decisions behind the UI.
Read time: 4 min
Read the Design Rationale ← Suite Overview

A dashboard that requires the user to think is a dashboard that gets ignored. Every structural decision — which KPI to show first, what to suppress, when to generate an action automatically — is an answer to the question: what does this person need to do next?

Design Philosophy

Three principles that governed
every metric choice

Before deciding what to measure, I defined the constraints: what makes a metric worth including, and what makes it worth leaving out.

01
Actionability over Completeness

A metric earns its place only if the viewer can act on it. Showing "total employee count" to a C-suite audience is noise — they can't change it by Friday. Showing "30-day operation risk" gives them something to approve or escalate. Every metric was pressure-tested against: what decision does this enable?

02
Audience-Specific Grain

The same underlying simulation produces three completely different dashboards. Executives see aggregated composite scores (OHI, EVP). HR managers see individual-level risk ranked by severity. Ops managers see shift-level flags. Same data, calibrated to the resolution at which each audience operates.

03
Causal Narrative, Not Correlation Gallery

Charts placed next to each other imply a relationship. The Executive KPI root-cause chart deliberately places labor cost ratio, coverage, and churn risk on the same timeline to make the causal chain visible: understaffing → churn → coverage decline → labor cost spike. The layout is the argument.

Designing for someone with 30 seconds and full authority

KPI Design

Why these four metrics — and not the obvious ones

The instinctive executive metrics are revenue, headcount, and costs. I rejected all three as primary KPIs because they are outputs, not levers. The dashboard uses leading indicators that precede P&L impact by 4–8 weeks.

Metric What It Measures Why Not the Simpler Alternative Decision Value
OHI
Org Health Index		 Coverage rate × (1 − avg churn risk). Composite of operational stability and people risk. Employee satisfaction score
Satisfaction surveys are lagging, self-reported, and don't connect to P&L. OHI is computed from behavioral signals.		 Single number that answers: "Is the organization healthy enough to execute next month's plan?"
EVP
Economic Value Protected		 Baseline loss (no action) minus optimized loss (with system interventions). Dollar value of risk avoided. Turnover cost YTD
Historical turnover cost is a post-mortem. EVP is forward-looking and quantifies the value of not acting versus acting.		 Justifies HR investment in dollar terms an executive understands without translation.
Labor Cost % Weekly labor cost ÷ weekly revenue. Compared against target (17.5%) and industry median (18.5%). Total weekly wage ($)
Absolute dollar amounts are meaningless without revenue context. A $14,000 wage bill is fine at $90K revenue and catastrophic at $55K.		 Directly links staffing decisions to margin. A delta of +2% triggers a specific approval workflow.
30-Day Op Risk Next month's forecast coverage vs. 90% threshold. Derived from Monte Carlo simulation with seasonal demand. Current week's understaffed shifts
Current-week data is too late — the shift already happened. A 30-day forward view gives executives time to approve budgets.		 Converts a staffing forecast into a binary risk signal executives can approve/escalate in one decision.

Information Hierarchy

The deliberate reading order

The layout forces a specific cognitive path: status → cause → action. Skipping any layer produces an uninformed decision.

1
Status — 4 Big Number Cards

Red / yellow / green color coding delivers the verdict in under 3 seconds. No chart-reading required. The executive knows whether to continue reading or escalate immediately. Each card shows only three data points: current value, benchmark, and delta.

OHI: 74.2% | Target: 80% | Delta: −5.8pp 🔴
2
Cause — KPI Root-Cause Chart

A single chart with three overlaid series: labor cost ratio (bars), shift coverage rate (green line), churn risk (red dashed). The layout embeds the causal argument — when the red line rises, the green line falls, and the bars spike 4–6 weeks later. No analytical work required; the relationship is visible.

Coverage ↓ in Aug → Churn ↑ in Sep → Labor Cost ↑ in Oct (Holiday backfill)
3
Trend — Cost vs. Risk Trend Chart

Past 12 months of actual labor cost (bars) + next 6 months forecast including the training/hiring plan effect (lighter bars) + revenue risk line (red dashed). The critical insight: "costs look controlled now, but the risk line spikes next quarter if no action is taken." The forecast period is what creates urgency.

Forecast: If current plan continues → Coverage drops to 83% in Nov → Est. $4,200 revenue risk
4
Action — Auto-Generated Executive Actions

Instead of asking the executive to derive actions from charts, the system generates specific, approvable items with dollar amounts and deadlines. Triggered by threshold crossings: OHI <80, EVP ≤0, LCR +2pp, coverage <85%, keyman churn ≥60%. Priority-sorted: 🔴 approve now, 🟡 schedule this month.

🔴 Approve hiring budget ($5,000) for Deli + Food (2 roles). Delay risks $4,200 revenue loss in November.

HR Manager Dashboard — People Operations
Designing for someone who needs to act on individuals, not averages

Layout Logic

Alert → Diagnose → Prescribe: why this order matters

HR managers are context-switchers. The dashboard must deliver the most time-sensitive information first, then provide the tools to investigate, then tell them exactly what to do. Reversing this order produces paralysis by analysis.

1
Alert — Top 3 Cards (the fires burning right now)

High Flight Risk count (with Advanced-skilled flag), 30-day Critical Skill Gap probability by department, and Hiring/Training needs summary. These are the three questions HR asks every Monday morning. The cards answer all three before the user scrolls.

🔴 High Flight Risk: 3 staff (incl. 1 Deli Advanced) | 30-Day Gap: Food 65% 🔴 | Needs: Hire 2 / Train 3
2
Diagnose — Talent Vulnerability Matrix

X-axis: Replaceability (skill uniqueness + cross-training depth). Y-axis: Retention probability (1 − churn risk). Bubble size: opportunity loss if this person leaves. The four quadrants answer different questions — 🚨 bottom-left demands immediate action; ✅ top-right needs no attention. Color-coded by role group so patterns emerge (e.g., all SLs clustering in the danger zone).

X-axis chosen over "years of service": tenure ≠ replaceability if skills are over-concentrated
Y-axis chosen over "engagement score": engagement surveys lag behavior by 4–8 weeks
3
Context — Stress & Turnover Trends (the backstory)

Weekly overtime vs. stress score (dual-axis chart), monthly termination/hire flow (bar chart). Placed after the matrix — not before — because these are explanatory, not alerting. When the HR manager sees a high-risk cluster in the matrix, they scroll down to see "oh, that department has had 3 consecutive high-stress weeks." Sequence creates narrative.

4
Prescribe — AI Prescriptive Actions (close the loop)

Three action types, each with a specific triggering condition: [1-on-1] when churn probability ≥50% + stress factor identified → generates a named, reason-explained recommendation. [Start Training] when skill gap >15% + peak season >4 weeks away → names the trainee candidate. [Hiring Alert] when coverage forecast drops below 90% within 6 months → names the shift type and deadline. The system closes the inference loop the HR manager would otherwise have to draw manually.

🗣️ [1-on-1] FT-03 (Food Adv) churn risk 72%. Root cause: 3 consecutive pref-mismatch weeks + OT overload. Schedule this week.

Matrix Design Decisions

Why these axes — not the obvious alternatives

X-axis: Why Replaceability?
Not performance score. Not years of service.

Performance score measures past contribution. Replaceability measures future risk. An employee can be a top performer and completely replaceable (cashier with 5 backups) — or a mediocre performer and irreplaceable (sole Deli Advanced holder). The business risk is concentrated where skill is scarce, not where performance is low.

Computed: skill uniqueness × cross-training depth
Y-axis: Why Retention Probability?
Not engagement score. Not manager rating.

Engagement surveys are self-reported, infrequent, and lag behavioral signals by 4–8 weeks. Retention probability is derived from LightGBM inference on 10 behavioral features updated every simulation run. It captures shift mismatch accumulation, overtime stress, and seasonal churn patterns that engagement surveys miss entirely.

Computed: 1 − LightGBM churn probability
Bubble Size: Why Opportunity Loss?
Not role seniority. Not salary band.

Seniority creates visual bias toward managers who may be easily replaceable. Opportunity loss — the revenue impact of this person leaving given current coverage gaps — makes the business consequence visible. A part-timer holding the last Deli Advanced skill during Holiday season has a larger bubble than the assistant manager they work under.

Computed: skill scarcity × seasonal demand × hire/train cost
4 Quadrants: Why These Thresholds?
0.5 replaceability / 0.5 retention: not arbitrary.

0.5 retention = 50% churn probability, which equals the historical industry average for retail annual turnover (FMI 2023: ~35–50% depending on role). At this threshold, the expected value of intervention breaks even with cost. 0.5 replaceability = average cross-training coverage. Below average on both axes defines the danger zone where intervention ROI is highest.

Threshold: FMI 2023 retail churn baseline

Ops Manager Dashboard — Shop Floor Operations
Designing for someone who acts in minutes, not days

Design Constraints

The ops manager needs flags, not scores

The critical insight governing this tab's design: ops managers make real-time decisions. A continuous score (e.g., "safety score: 47") is not actionable at 9am when a shift is about to start. A flag (⚠️) on a specific skill × shift combination is. Every metric was redesigned from continuous to categorical where possible.

Safety Score: Why target headcount as denominator?
Not actual headcount. Not percentage of ideal.

Using actual headcount makes understaffed shifts appear more competent than they are. If target is 6 and only 2 show up — both Advanced — the score would be 100% with actual count as denominator. With target count, the score reflects the real risk: 2 people doing the work of 6, regardless of their skill level.

Safety Score = Σ(skill_level × weight) / target_headcount
Coverage chart: Why separate Advanced from Basic?
Not a single "coverage rate." Skill-level disaggregation.

A shift fully covered by Basic-level staff in the Deli department has a fundamentally different risk profile than one covered by Advanced staff. Aggregating into a single coverage number masks the skill depth problem. The bar charts show Advanced hit-rate and Basic hit-rate separately, allowing the ops manager to immediately see whether they have people or capable people.

Disaggregated: Advanced hit-rate | Basic hit-rate | per skill × shift
Satisfaction ranking: Why sorted ascending?
Worst first. Not alphabetical. Not by role.

When sorted by satisfaction ascending, the people who need a follow-up conversation appear at the top without any scanning. The ops manager opens the dashboard and immediately sees the three staff members whose shifts deviated most from their preferences. Sorting by role or name requires cognitive work to find the at-risk individuals. The ⚠ icon on specific cells surfaces the exact day/shift where the deviation occurred.

Sort: satisfaction score ascending (worst at top)
Substitute Suggestion: Why show soft-NG candidates?
Not excluded — flagged with ⚠. Transparent tradeoffs.

Excluding soft-NG (preferred-rest) candidates produces a cleaner list but less information. In an emergency absence, the manager may need to contact someone on their preferred day off. By including them with an ⚠ flag, the tool respects the manager's authority to make that call while making the tradeoff visible. Hard constraints (labor law, max hours) remain absolute exclusions.

Rank: skill match → hours margin → wage. Soft-NG: flagged, not excluded.

Shift Stress Analysis

Why composite stress — and not just overtime hours

Overtime hours alone miss two critical stressors that predict burnout better in retail: skill scarcity pressure (being the only person who can do something) and preference mismatch accumulation (being repeatedly assigned shifts you hate). The composite score weights three components:

Shift Stress Score Composition
Component
Formula
Design Rationale
Shortage Stress (×0.5)
shortage_ratio × role_burden × day_type × skill_scarcity
Highest weight because it directly predicts burnout. MG/ASM get 1.8× role multiplier (decision load increases when understaffed). Sale days get 1.4× (peak demand amplifies every stressor).
Preference Stress (×0.2)
mismatch_days / total_days. For "Any" preference: shift variety as proxy (max 0.3 cap).
Lower weight because a single mismatch is acceptable; accumulation is the problem. "Any" preference staff still experience stress from being rotated through all shifts — captured via variance proxy.
Overtime Risk (×0.3)
avg_shortage × 0.6 + tenure_factor × 0.2 + (1 − engagement/100) × 0.2. MG/ASM/SL only.
Shorter tenure = harder to refuse overtime (0.9 factor). Longer tenure = more leverage to decline (0.3). Engagement drop correlates with refusal tendency but also with the stress of being asked repeatedly.

Cross-Audience Design

One simulation.
Three completely different stories.

The same Monte Carlo simulation result feeds all three dashboards. The transformation applied at each layer — what is aggregated, what is surfaced, what is suppressed — defines the analytical design skill. Here is how the same data becomes three different answers.

🏦 Executive
Input
MC simulation: adv_hit_rate by skill × shift
Transformation
Aggregated into OHI (avg_coverage × risk factor). Trend line extended 6 months with seasonal multipliers.
Output
Single composite number + color signal
"Is the org healthy? Yes/No."
Suppressed
Individual skill breakdowns, shift-level flags, specific employee names
👥 HR Manager
Input
MC simulation: adv_hit_rate by skill × shift
Transformation
Converted to skill gap probability per department. Combined with LightGBM churn scores per individual.
Output
Named individuals ranked by risk × replaceability
"Who do I call first this week?"
Suppressed
Shift-level granularity, wage cost breakdown, substitute ranking
🏪 Ops Manager
Input
MC simulation: adv_hit_rate by skill × shift
Transformation
Kept at full resolution: skill × shift × day_type. Safety score computed per cell. Binary ⚠ flag at threshold.
Output
⚠ flags on specific shifts. Ranked substitute list.
"Which slot do I fix right now?"
Suppressed
Aggregate scores, trend lines, individual churn probability

The Design Insight

The same 10,000 simulation results are used in all three tabs — zero additional computation. The analytical work is entirely in the transformation layer: what level of abstraction does each audience need? Executives need convergence (one number). HR managers need individual resolution (names). Ops managers need spatial resolution (which slot, which skill). Mismatching resolution to audience is why most dashboards fail.

Insight Extraction Framework

The "So What?" test
applied to every metric

Every metric in the dashboard passed a four-stage filter before inclusion. This is the same framework I apply when evaluating whether a number belongs on a dashboard or in an appendix.

01
What is this measuring?

Precise definition of the numerator, denominator, and time window. Ambiguous metrics produce arguments, not decisions.

OHI = avg_coverage × (1 − avg_churn_risk). Not "employee wellbeing." Not "morale."
02
Who does it change behavior for?

If the answer isn't a specific role, the metric belongs in a data warehouse, not a dashboard. Metrics without an owner are decoration.

30-Day Op Risk → Owner: C-Suite. They have budget authority to approve emergency hiring. HR Manager cannot approve budget.
03
What does "bad" look like — specifically?

A metric without a threshold is a number without meaning. Thresholds must be calibrated to the business, not set arbitrarily.

LCR: Target 17.5% (our margin model). Warning at +2pp (triggers P&L review). Based on FMI 2023 median 18.5%.
04
What is the next action?

If crossing the threshold doesn't trigger a specific, named action within a defined timeframe, the metric is a vanity metric regardless of how interesting it is.

OHI < 60% → "Approve emergency staffing budget this week." Not "investigate further."

What Didn't Make the Cut

Rejected metrics — and why

Deciding what not to show is as important as deciding what to show. These were removed during design iteration.

Rejected Metric Why It Felt Useful Why It Was Cut
Employee satisfaction score (survey) Directly measures how people feel about work. Lagging by 4–8 weeks. Self-reported. Not correlated with behavioral churn signals in this dataset. Replaced by LightGBM churn probability derived from operational data.
Total headcount Executives always want to know "how many people do we have." Headcount without skill composition is meaningless. 30 employees with no Deli Advanced coverage is worse than 25 with full skill depth. Replaced by skill coverage hit-rates.
Average tenure Longer tenure = more experience = lower risk. Tenure doesn't predict churn (long-tenured employees exit too, just for different reasons). Doesn't capture skill depth or replaceability. Used as a feature inside the churn model, not as a standalone dashboard metric.
Training completion rate (%) Shows whether the training plan is on track. Percentage completed is an activity metric, not an outcome metric. 100% completion of the wrong training plan is worse than 0% of the right one. Replaced by coverage rate change post-training, which measures actual skill uplift.

What This Demonstrates

Dashboard design
as analytical discipline

🎯
KPI Architecture

Composite metrics (OHI, EVP) designed from first principles, with documented rationale for every component weight. Threshold calibration against industry benchmarks (FMI 2023), not intuition.

🧭
Audience-Resolution Matching

The deliberate choice to aggregate, disaggregate, or flag the same data depending on who is reading it — and the ability to explain why each level of abstraction serves its audience.

🔍
Insightful Metric Rejection

Removing metrics that feel useful but don't pass the actionability test. Survey satisfaction, total headcount, and training completion rate all failed the "so what?" filter — and are documented as intentional exclusions, not oversights.

📖
Layout as Argument

The sequence of charts in the Executive tab — cards → root cause → trend → action — is a structured argument, not an aesthetic choice. Each section answers the question the previous one raises.
