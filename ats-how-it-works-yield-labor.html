Engine 04 — Technical Deep Dive

How the Yield & Labor Simulator works

A plain-English explanation of Monte Carlo simulation — sampling 2,000 weather scenarios to generate P10/P50/P90 revenue distributions and probabilistic labor-gap alerts for a 21.5-hectare Hawaii farm.

Instead of a single "expected yield" number, this engine runs 2,000 random weather draws per month — each one independently computing crop yields, revenues, and labor demand — then summarizes the distribution as pessimistic (P10), median (P50), and optimistic (P90) forecasts so the farm owner can plan staffing and finance with known risk levels.

The Technology

Monte Carlo + probabilistic weather sampling

Monte Carlo simulation — the core loop

2,000-trial stochastic sampling

For each of 2,000 trials, the engine independently samples a weather scenario from the probability distribution for every month. Within each scenario, Gaussian noise is added to all weather parameters (rainfall ±25%, temperature ±1.5°C, humidity ±8%, pest risk ±20%) to model within-scenario variability. The trial then computes yield, revenue, and labor demand for all 5 crops. After all trials, P10/P50/P90 percentiles are extracted from the 2,000-value distribution — giving a statistically grounded range rather than a single-point estimate.

Multi-factor yield response function

Crop-specific weather sensitivity model

Each crop has a weather_sensitivity coefficient (papaya=0.8, coffee=0.6, taro=0.5, macadamia=0.4, pineapple=0.5). Three sub-factors are computed independently — rainfall_factor (optimal 60–120 mm/month), temp_factor (optimal 22–27°C), and pest_factor — then combined: combined = 1 − sensitivity × (1 − rain × temp × pest). This captures that a drought-resistant macadamia orchard barely changes under dry spells while papaya production collapses. Revenue adjusts price upward by +10% for every 10% demand above baseline, modelling tourism-driven price surges.

Yield & Revenue calculation per trial

# For each trial t, month m, crop c: scenario = sample_from_distribution(probs) # weighted draw weather = add_gaussian_noise(scenario) # ±25% rainfall, ±1.5°C temp, ... rain_factor = clamp(f(rainfall), 0.2, 1.2) # optimal 60–120 mm temp_factor = max(0.7, 1 − 0.03 × |temp − 24.5|) pest_factor = max(0.5, 1 − 0.15 × (pest − 1.0)) combined = 1 − sensitivity[c] × (1 − rain × temp × pest) yield_kg = base_yield_kg_ha[c] × area_ha / 12 × combined adj_price = price_per_kg[c] × (1 + 0.1 × (demand_factor − 1)) revenue = yield_kg × adj_price # Labor demand = fixed base + harvest variable labor = area_ha × 0.25 + yield_kg / labor_kg_per_month[c]

After 2,000 trials per month: P10/P50/P90 = sorted_results[200], [1000], [1800]. Labor shortage probability = count(labor > 9 workers) / 2000.

Data Pipeline

How data flows through the simulator

  1. Live weather fetch from Open-Meteo API

    On session start the engine queries Open-Meteo's historical/forecast endpoints for Waimea (Big Island north), Hilo (east/wet side), and Kona (west/dry side). Recent rainfall, temperature, and wind patterns adjust the base scenario probabilities — e.g., a dry run-up raises the drought probability above 20%.

  2. Farm configuration & worker count from session_state

    User-adjustable sliders for per-crop area (ha), current full-time workers, and seasonal hire cap are read from Streamlit session_state. Any changes trigger a fresh simulation run with the new parameters.

  3. 2,000-trial Monte Carlo loop

    For each trial: sample scenario → add noise → compute yield/revenue/labor for all 5 crops across 12 months. Results are accumulated into per-month raw arrays. Uses a seeded random.Random(42) for reproducibility; seed is user-adjustable in advanced settings.

  4. Percentile extraction & risk alert generation

    P10/P50/P90 are extracted from sorted arrays. Labor shortage probability is computed as count(labor_demand > current_workers) / 2000. Months with >80% shortage probability trigger HIGH severity alerts; >50% trigger MEDIUM. Recommended seasonal hire = ceil(max_labor_p90 − current_workers).

  5. Output to Harvest Logistics & Knowledge Transfer engines

    The monthly risk_alerts array and recommended_seasonal_hire are pushed to session_state. The Harvest Logistics Optimizer uses shortage months to flag scheduling stress periods. The Knowledge Transfer Planner uses task_failure_probs from stress tests to prioritize urgent OJT sessions.

Results

What the simulation produces

Monthly P10 / P50 / P90 revenue bands

A fan chart showing the pessimistic, median, and optimistic revenue outcome for each month — directly answering "what's the worst-case month for cash flow planning?"

Labor shortage probability per month

A probability bar (0–100%) showing how often the 9 full-time workers are insufficient. Combined with the coffee harvest peak (Sep–Jan) and macadamia season (Aug–Dec) overlapping, this reveals the critical staffing crunch months.

Recommended seasonal hire count

ceil(max_labor_p90 − 9) gives the number of seasonal workers needed to cover the worst-case labor demand at the 90th percentile — with a 3-week recruitment lead time flag if the crunch is within the window.

Opportunity loss estimate

annual_revenue_p50 − actual_revenue_p50 quantifies the revenue lost when labor shortages prevent full harvest. This is the key number used to justify seasonal worker hiring costs.

Per-scenario comparison table

run_scenario_comparison() re-runs 500 trials at 100% probability for each of the 5 scenarios individually — giving a side-by-side comparison of annual yield, revenue, peak labor, and shortage months under each extreme.

Why It Matters

The farm management problem it solves

From "hope for the best" to risk-quantified planning

Traditional Hawaii farm planning uses last year's numbers. Monte Carlo replaces hindsight with forward-looking probability distributions — the farm owner can see that there's a 30% chance next January revenue drops below $X before committing to equipment loans.

Seasonal hire decisions with ROI justification

The opportunity loss figure ($revenue lost due to understaffing) vs. the cost of hiring seasonal workers creates a direct ROI calculation — replacing gut feeling with quantified break-even analysis for HR decisions.

Hawaii-specific multi-crop complexity

With 5 crops across 21.5 ha each having different harvest seasons, weather sensitivities, and price points, the interactions are too complex to track manually. The simulator captures all cross-crop effects simultaneously — e.g., coffee and macadamia peak labor demand coinciding.

Pipeline input for downstream engines

The simulator's output — shortage months, task failure probabilities, recommended hires — feeds directly into the Harvest Logistics Optimizer and Knowledge Transfer Planner. The entire suite's risk response is calibrated by what the Monte Carlo simulation reveals each run.
