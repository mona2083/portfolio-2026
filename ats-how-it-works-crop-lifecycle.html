How the Crop Lifecycle Manager works

A plain-English explanation of the GDD thermal accumulation model and water balance engine that tracks every planted batch from germination to harvest.

Instead of relying on a calendar date, this engine tracks actual biological progress — accumulating heat units day by day, factoring in soil water deficit, Kona wind stress, and pest pressure, to predict the real harvest window for each parcel.

The Technology

Biophysical modelling, not calendar-based guessing

Proven agronomic science

Growing Degree Day (GDD) Model

GDD is the standard agronomic measure of thermal accumulation. A crop grows when temperature exceeds its base temperature (T_base). Each day contributes (avg_temp − T_base) GDD. When cumulative GDD crosses a threshold, the crop advances to the next growth stage. This is more accurate than calendar-based forecasting because the same crop planted in different years may mature weeks apart due to temperature variation.

Multi-factor stress compounding

Water Balance + Wind + Pest Stress Model

Raw GDD is multiplied by a stress_factor (0–1) computed from three independent stressors. Soil moisture deficit below the "dry" threshold reduces effective GDD by 20%; below "drought" by 50%. Kona wind days (Hawaii's dry trade winds) apply a crop-specific wind multiplier. Pest history score (0–1) applies up to a maximum growth penalty per crop. All three compound multiplicatively.

The Math

Daily GDD calculation

# Raw GDD for one day raw_gdd = max(0.0, avg_temp_c − base_temp_c[crop]) # Stress factor (multiplicative compounding) stress = 1.0 if soil_moisture < drought_threshold: stress × = 0.50 # extreme drought elif soil_moisture < dry_threshold: stress × = 0.80 # mild drought if kona_wind_days ≥ 3: stress × = wind_stress_multiplier[crop] # Papaya = 0.80 | Macadamia = 0.85 | Coffee = 0.90 # Taro = 0.98 | Pineapple = 1.00 (ground-hugging, wind-immune) pest_penalty = pest_history_score × pest_stress_max_penalty[crop] stress × = (1.0 − pest_penalty) # Effective daily GDD actual_gdd = raw_gdd × stress # Stage transition cumulative_gdd += actual_gdd if cumulative_gdd ≥ stage_threshold[crop][next_stage]: → advance stage

Stress Parameters

Water & pest thresholds per crop

WATER STRESS

Taro — highest water demand

Dry threshold: 50% soil moisture. Drought threshold: 35%. Taro is a wetland crop — moisture deficits immediately reduce GDD accumulation. Even mild dryness triggers 20% growth reduction.

WATER STRESS

Papaya — sensitive to drought

Dry threshold: 35%. Drought threshold: 20%. Combined with its high wind sensitivity (×0.80), a Kona wind week during dry conditions can compound to 36% growth reduction (0.80 × 0.80 = 0.64).

PEST STRESS

Papaya — ringspot virus risk

Max pest penalty: 30%. Papaya ringspot virus is endemic to Hawaii. A pest score of 1.0 reduces daily GDD by up to 30%. Accumulated through the season, a high pest year can delay harvest by 3–4 weeks.

PEST STRESS

Coffee — moderate pest risk

Max pest penalty: 20%. Coffee berry borer is the primary concern. Combined with its already-long 2,500 GDD requirement, a high-pest year can shift the harvest window by 2–3 weeks — critical for hand-picking coordination.

Data Pipeline

How lifecycle data flows downstream

  1. Receives initial batches from Planting Optimizer

    When the Planting Strategy Optimizer runs, new planting events for unoccupied parcels are written to session_state["batches"] — each with crop type, planted_date, and initial GDD (perennials start at 800 GDD to reflect existing tree maturity).

  2. Manual weather inputs advance batches

    In the app, the user sets today's avg_temp, soil_moisture, Kona wind days, and pest score. The GrowthEngine calculates daily GDD for each parcel and updates cumulative totals. Stage transitions are detected automatically.

  3. Harvest-ready batches flagged for Health Predictor

    Batches entering the "harvest" stage are written to session_state["harvest_ready_parcels"]. The Crop Health Predictor reads this list to prioritize which parcels need quality risk assessment.

  4. Stage data feeds Portal COO view

    The Field Manager (COO) portal reads batch stage distribution to show what proportion of the farm is in each growth stage — a key indicator for upcoming labor demand.

Results

What the app shows

① Parcel status grid

Each parcel shown as a card: crop type, current stage, GDD progress bar (current / target), health status (good / stressed / critical), and days since last update.

② GDD accumulation timeline

Line chart showing cumulative GDD over time per batch, with stage threshold lines overlaid. Stressed periods show as a shallower slope — visually showing the cost of water or pest stress.

③ Stress factor breakdown

Per-parcel bar chart decomposing today's effective GDD into: base GDD, water stress penalty, wind penalty, pest penalty. Managers can see exactly which stressor is slowing growth most.

④ Stage transition alerts

Batches that advanced to a new stage today are highlighted with a banner. Batches entering "harvest" stage trigger a cross-app alert visible in the Field Manager portal.

⑤ Estimated harvest date

Based on current GDD accumulation rate (rolling 7-day average), the engine projects the date when each batch will reach harvest GDD — accounting for current stress factors continuing at today's level.

Why It Matters

What this demonstrates

🌱
Agronomic Science as Code

GDD-based growth modelling is the gold standard in precision agriculture. Implementing it as a per-parcel, per-day calculation with multi-factor stress attenuation demonstrates domain depth beyond surface-level data visualization.

🏝️
Hawaii-Specific Parameters

Kona wind multipliers, island crop-specific base temperatures, and pest profiles (papaya ringspot, coffee berry borer) are calibrated to Big Island conditions — not generic tropical farm defaults.

🔗
Bidirectional Pipeline

Receives planting data from Engine 01 upstream; sends harvest-readiness signals to the Crop Health Predictor and Field Manager portal downstream. The Lifecycle Manager is the biological backbone of the entire suite.

📈
Proactive vs. Reactive Management

Rather than waiting for visual defects to appear, this engine gives managers advance warning — days before harvest stage is reached — so labor and logistics can be pre-arranged while quality is still optimal.
